# Student Prompt Do's and Don'ts

Concrete before/after pairs for the leak-free prompt rule. Add new pairs here
whenever a leaky prompt slips through in practice — this file is the skill's
institutional memory for this failure mode.

## Pair 1 — Evaluative framing leak

**BAD:**
> With this powerful 18-point hand and great spade support, what is your bid
> after partner opens 1♠?

Leaks: "powerful", "great spade support" — the evaluation is the exercise.

**GOOD:**
> Partner opens 1♠. What is your call?

The student sees their own cards. Say nothing about them.

## Pair 2 — Answer word in the question

**BAD:**
> Partner opens 1NT. Should you transfer to hearts, or is there a better
> option?

Leaks: names the transfer, telegraphing that a transfer is on the table and
hearts is the relevant suit.

**GOOD:**
> Partner opens 1NT. What is your call?

## Pair 3 — Leading auction commentary

**BAD:**
> Partner's 2♣ response shows a strong hand, so you need to keep the bidding
> open. What do you bid?

Leaks: the entire reasoning chain — the answer is now a fill-in-the-blank.

**GOOD:**
> The auction so far: 1♠ – 2♣ – ? What is your call?

If interpreting 2♣ is itself a previously-taught skill, trust the student to
apply it. If it hasn't been taught yet, this deal doesn't belong in the lesson.

## Pair 4 — Intro paragraph leak

**BAD (lesson intro):**
> In this lesson you'll practice when to open 1NT. All eight boards feature
> balanced hands of 15–17 points.

Leaks: tells the student the answer to every board before board 1.

**GOOD (lesson intro):**
> In this lesson you'll practice choosing an opening bid. For each board,
> decide on your call before revealing the answer.

The theme can be revealed in the lesson SUMMARY, after the boards.

## Pair 5 — Structural leak

**BAD:** Eight boards, and the correct answer to "should you invite game?" is
"yes" on all eight.

**GOOD:** Mix in boards where the correct call is Pass or a simple raise. The
skill being taught is the decision, not the action.
