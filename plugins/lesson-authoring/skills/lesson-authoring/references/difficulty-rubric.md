# Board Difficulty Rubric (1–5)

Rate every board before sequencing a lesson. Rate relative to the AUDIENCE of
the lesson, not to an expert player. A board that is trivial for an
intermediate may be a 4 for a beginner three weeks into the course.

**1 — Direct rule application.** The taught rule applies immediately with no
competing considerations. Textbook shapes, point counts comfortably inside
ranges, no interference. Example: 16 HCP balanced, no 5-card major → open 1NT.

**2 — Rule application with one wrinkle.** The rule applies but the student
must notice one thing first (a 5-card major to check for, a borderline-looking
but actually clear point count, choosing between two obvious suits by length).

**3 — Judgment inside the rule.** Values at the edge of a range requiring
standard evaluation (a good/bad 14), choice between two calls resolved by a
taught tiebreaker, or a simple competitive auction where the taught rule still
governs.

**4 — Multiple interacting concepts.** The board requires combining two or more
taught skills, or recognizing which of several rules applies. Suitable late in
a lesson, or as the stretch board.

**5 — Expert territory.** Genuine multi-way judgment, inference from the
auction, or deception considerations. Almost never belongs in a beginner
lesson. If a repository deal rates 5 for the target audience, discard or
repair it rather than including it.

## Sequencing rules

- Sort boards by rating, ties broken to keep thematic variety adjacent boards
  from feeling repetitive.
- Beginner lessons: first third must be rated 1–2; no board rated above 4;
  at most one 4, placed last.
- If a lesson's ratings would come out flat (all the same), that's a signal to
  diversify deal selection, not to fake a gradient.
